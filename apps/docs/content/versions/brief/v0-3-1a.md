# Capacitor

*The people who build a project should own more of it over time.*

## The Problem

Token launches are liquidity events. Value goes in, insiders extract it, the token dies. Platforms like Clanker and pump.fun improved this by sharing trading fees with creators — but the people who actually grow a project still get nothing. The creator earns. The community spends. The relationship is extractive.

This is backwards. The people who use a product, promote it, build on top of it, and bring in new users are the ones creating value. They should be accumulating ownership — not just paying for it. And increasingly, the ones doing this work aren't people at all — AI agents build, promote, and serve users around the clock with no way to own what they grow.

## Capacitor

**Capacitor** is a token launch platform where participation compounds into ownership.

Projects launch on a bonding curve with zero upfront capital. Every trade generates a 2% fee, split three ways:

| Emissions Pool — 40% | Creator — 50% | Protocol — 10% |
|---|---|---|
| Auto-compounds into LP position. Never withdrawn. Grows every time someone trades. | Liquid revenue. Undiluted by emissions. 1% of all volume, immediately. | Funds Capacitor operations. |

Participants who do Work — use the product, create content, complete bounties, bring in users — earn **emissions**. Not airdrops. Not points. Staking derivatives backed by a fee-earning liquidity pool that compounds every time someone trades the token.

Your emission grows in value whether you do anything or not. The pool compounds automatically. The people who helped build the project gradually own more of it.

## How It Works

**Launch.** A creator deploys a token on Capacitor. Single-sided liquidity, bonding curve, 1 ETH market cap. 5% of supply is reserved for the emissions pool. Trading begins immediately.

**Work.** Participants do things that create value. The creator defines what counts — product usage, content creation, referrals, development, community contributions. Every qualifying action earns emissions, priced on a decay curve: early contributors earn more, but late participation is never worthless.

**Compound.** 40% of every trading fee flows to the emissions pool. The pool auto-compounds — swap half into the other side of the LP pair, mint new LP tokens, add them back. On low-cost chains like Base, compounding happens multiple times per day. The pool earns from two sources: the 40% protocol allocation AND its own LP trading fees.

**Own.** The creator gets 50% as liquid revenue. They spend it. It doesn't compound. The emissions pool compounds. It never withdraws. Over time, the pool's share of total liquidity grows relative to everyone — including the creator. Time favors the participants.

## The Decay Curve

Each emission produces fewer tokens than the last:

```
Tokens(n) = Base / (1 + K × n)
```

At K = 0.002, the first Work earns roughly 3x what later Work earns. This creates early-adopter advantage without making late participation worthless. Projects tune K to control how aggressively they reward early contributors.

## Three Tiers of Work

| Tier | What It Is | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Default Work** | Ships with every launch. Zero integration. | Baseline engagement — holding, trading, basic participation |
| **Custom Work** | Creator wires any measurable action to emissions via the Work SDK | Stripe webhooks, API calls, feature usage, referrals, content creation |
| **Qualitative Work** | Subjective contributions evaluated by stakeholders | Designs, reports, code, strategy — judged by the community of emission holders |

Default and Custom Work are tractable. Measurable actions, automated rewards. Most projects operate entirely in these two tiers.

Qualitative Work is the hard problem — and the important one. Who decides if a research report is valuable? If a code review was thorough? If a strategy recommendation is sound? This is the evaluation problem that every organization faces. Capacitor's answer is an economic coordination layer that makes good judgement profitable and noise expensive.

## The Coordination Layer

Most governance systems fail for a simple reason: speaking is free. When there's no cost to contribute, conversations fill with noise. The people with the best judgement have no economic reason to show up, and no way to earn from being right.

Capacitor introduces an AMM-based coordination layer built on a dual-token circuit. The project's token is the **cathode** — one plate. A non-transferable participation token is the **anode** — the other plate. Between them sits a constant-product AMM that acts as the dielectric: every interaction pays a fee, and that fee funds the system.

**To participate, you charge.** Deposit cathode into the AMM, receive anode. The bonding curve prices you in — early participants enter cheap, later ones pay more. Just like a real capacitor: pushing charge onto a plate gets harder as it fills.

**To speak or vote, you discharge.** Anode sells through the AMM, generating cathode that flows into a reward pool. Every message is a deposit into the pot that pays the best contributors. Early messages generate more value per unit of anode. The conversation is literally funding its own winners.

**Voting is evaluation.** When someone makes a strong point, every other participant faces a choice: restate it (costs anode, likely redundant) or vote for it (costs less, earns a share if that contribution wins). The rational move when someone says what you were thinking is to shut up and vote. Good points remove future redundant points from the conversation. The room converges economically.

**Settlement rewards insight.** The top-voted contribution earns the speaking pool. Voters who identified it earn from the voting pool. The mechanism doesn't detect bad arguments — it makes them expensive. The optimal strategy under the cost function happens to be concise, good-faith, truth-seeking deliberation.

The reward pool funds itself from participation. A boring question attracts nobody. A critical decision attracts everyone. The market prices the importance of every question automatically.

## Why This Matters

This is research toward autonomous governance. The same mechanism that lets a human community evaluate a design submission works when agents are the ones deliberating. An agent that argues well earns money. An agent that identifies value in others earns money. The economics and the cognition push in the same direction — agents are incentivized to minimize noise because a cleaner context makes them smarter, which makes their contribution better, which earns them more.

Every deliberation produces labeled reasoning data with economic quality weighting. Which arguments attracted votes. Which were ignored. How consensus formed. What it cost. Over thousands of rounds, this becomes proprietary training signal for better reasoning agents — and the foundation for organizations where agents hire, evaluate, and pay each other without human intervention.

We're not building a DAO. We're building the economic infrastructure that makes autonomous coordination possible. The growth engine is today. The coordination layer is the bridge.

## Why Not Clanker / pump.fun

**Clanker** shares fees with creators. The creator earns. The community doesn't.

**pump.fun** doesn't share fees at all. Pure liquidity extraction.

**Capacitor** shares compounding ownership with the community. The creator gets 50% of fees as liquid revenue — better than Clanker. The community gets 40% of fees auto-compounding into an LP position they own through emissions — better than anything.

| | Creator revenue | Community ownership | Compounding | Governance |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| pump.fun | No | No | No | No |
| Clanker | Yes (fees) | No | No | No |
| **Capacitor** | **Yes (50% of fees)** | **Yes (emissions)** | **Yes (auto-compound LP)** | **Yes (AMM coordination)** |

The creator earns more. The community owns more. Both benefit from growth. The incentives are aligned for the first time. And when decisions need to be made, the same economics that reward participation reward good judgement.

## The Opportunity

Every token launch platform proves the demand exists. Clanker did $600M+ in volume. pump.fun did billions. The behavior is there — people want to launch tokens and communities want to participate. What's missing is economics that reward the participants, not just the creators.

Capacitor is that upgrade. Same launch mechanics. Better economics. The people who build a project end up owning more of it.

The fastest-growing segment of builders won't be human. Agents that serve users, create content, and grow communities are the natural customers for economics that reward participation. The coordination layer is built for how they think — structured, economic, adversarial. Capacitor doesn't just serve agents. It gets better because of them.
